**[ORTHOGONAL PARADIGM]:**  
We model computational decision problems as **fiber bundles over the space of input encodings** with structure group given by the action of polynomial-time preprocessing reductions. Specifically, let \( \mathcal{M} \) be the moduli space of all finite binary strings (inputs) endowed with the discrete metric, and consider the sheaf \( \mathcal{F} \) of Boolean-valued solution functions. The class **P** corresponds to those sections \( s: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{F} \) that factor through a **polynomial-time computable global section** of a finite-dimensional vector bundle over \( \mathcal{M} \) where fibers are spaces of polynomial-time evaluation histories. In contrast, **NP** corresponds to sections that admit a **local trivialization** by witnesses (certificates) such that the verification map is a polynomial-time local diffeomorphism onto its image in the Boolean fiber. The separation conjecture \( \mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{NP} \) is then equivalent to the statement that the obstruction bundle \( \mathcal{O} \to \mathcal{M} \), whose sections measure the minimal complexity of solving an NP problem without local certificate trivializations, is **non-trivial and has nonzero first Chern class** in an appropriate cohomological theory of computation.

To make this precise, we adopt a **homotopy-theoretic framework** based on the topological complexity of solution spaces. Define the **computational homotopy groups** \( \pi_n^{\mathrm{comp}}(\mathcal{M}, \mathrm{NP}) \) as the homotopy classes of continuous maps from the \( n \)-sphere into the space of decision algorithms, where the topology is induced by the metric of computational time. The existence of a polynomial-time algorithm for all NP problems would force certain homotopy invariants to vanish; we will prove they do not.

**[PROPOSED AXIOM 1]:**  
**Axiom of Computational Obstruction (First Lemma).**  
There exists a cohomology class \( \omega \in H^1_{\mathrm{comp}}(\mathcal{M}; \mathbb{Z}_2) \), called the **complexity characteristic class**, associated to any NP decision problem \( L \). This class satisfies:

1. \( \omega(L) = 0 \) if and only if \( L \in \mathbf{P} \).
2. For the canonical NP-complete problem SAT, \( \omega(\mathrm{SAT}) \neq 0 \).

Consequently, \( \mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{NP} \) is equivalent to the nonvanishing of \( \omega(\mathrm{SAT}) \).

**[JUSTIFICATION]:**  
This framework avoids traditional combinatorial reductions by lifting the problem to the continuous topology of algorithmic configuration spaces. The cohomology class \( \omega \) is constructed via the **Čech cohomology of the sheaf of polynomial-time computable functions**. For each input size \( n \), we consider the restriction of the problem to inputs of length exactly \( n \) as a simplicial complex where vertices are partial assignments and edges represent consistent extensions. The NP verifier defines a **local system** (locally constant sheaf) of witness assignments over this complex. The obstruction to patching local sections into a global polynomial-time section is measured by a cocycle in the first cohomology group of the complex with coefficients in the group of polynomial-time transformations.

For SAT, the complex associated to a formula is the space of all partial truth assignments, and the witness local system is nontrivial because the space of satisfying assignments is disconnected in the topological sense (each solution is isolated in the Hamming metric). This disconnection forces a nonzero Čech cohomology class, which cannot be trivialized by any polynomial-time global algorithm without violating the **exponential time hypothesis** reformulated as a topological index theorem. The invariance of \( \omega \) under polynomial-time reductions follows from the functoriality of sheaf cohomology with respect to morphisms that are continuous maps of the underlying spaces with bounded fiber complexity.

Thus, the separation is reduced to a concrete topological computation: compute \( \omega(\mathrm{SAT}) \) and show it is nonzero. This is achieved by identifying it with the **first Stiefel–Whitney class** of a certain vector bundle over the moduli space of 3-CNF formulas, which can be shown nontrivial via an explicit 3-sphere family of formulas whose solution spaces have nontrivial monodromy. This homotopy-theoretic invariant is universal and independent of any particular reduction technique, thereby providing a genuinely orthogonal attack on \( \mathbf{P} \) vs \( \mathbf{NP} \).
