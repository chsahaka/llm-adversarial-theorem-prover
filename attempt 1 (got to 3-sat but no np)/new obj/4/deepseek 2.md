**[ALGEBRAIC GEOMETRY PARADIGM]:**  
We work over the algebraically closed field \(\overline{\mathbb{F}}_2\). Let \(L \subseteq \{0,1\}^*\) be a decision problem. For each input length \(n\), let \(N = \text{poly}(n)\) be the number of bits encoding an instance, and let \(L_N \subseteq \mathbb{F}_2^N\) be the set of YES instances.  

- **NP as projection:** There exists a polynomial \(m(N)\) and an affine variety \(V_N \subseteq \mathbb{A}^{N+m(N)}\) defined by polynomials \(f_1,\dots,f_r\) of degree at most 3 (arising from the verifier's circuit equations) such that \(L_N = \pi_N(V_N(\mathbb{F}_2))\), where \(\pi_N: \mathbb{A}^{N+m} \to \mathbb{A}^N\) is the linear projection forgetting the witness coordinates.  
- **P as sequential polynomial maps:** A language \(L\) is in **P** if there exists a polynomial \(t(N)\) and a sequence of polynomial maps \(\Phi_1,\dots,\Phi_{t(N)}: \mathbb{F}_2^{M} \to \mathbb{F}_2^{M}\) (with \(M = \text{poly}(N)\)) where each \(\Phi_i\) has **total degree at most 2** (encoding a single Turing machine step), such that the characteristic function of \(L_N\) is given by the first coordinate of \(\Phi_{t(N)} \circ \cdots \circ \Phi_1\) applied to a fixed starting configuration. In geometric terms, \(L_N\) is the image of an affine linear subspace under a **degree-\(2^{\text{poly}(N)}\)** polynomial map—but more importantly, it is **constructible by a polynomial-length composition of quadratic maps**.

The separation hinges on the **elimination complexity** of the ideal of the Zariski closure \(\overline{L_N} \subseteq \mathbb{A}^N\).

**[PROPOSED STEP 1]:**  
**Lemma (Elimination Ideal Degree Lower Bound for SAT).**  
Let \(I_N \subseteq \overline{\mathbb{F}}_2[x_1,\dots,x_N]\) be the ideal of all polynomials vanishing on the Zariski closure of the set of satisfiable 3-CNF formulas of size \(N\) (under a natural encoding). Then for infinitely many \(N\), any set of generators for \(I_N\) contains a polynomial whose **total degree** is at least \(2^{\Omega(N^{1/3})}\). In contrast, for any language \(L \in \mathbf{P}\), the ideal of \(\overline{L_N}\) admits a set of generators of degree at most \(\text{poly}(N)\) and, moreover, the total number of monomials across all generators is bounded by a polynomial in \(N\).

Consequently, SAT cannot be in **P** because its algebraic description requires super-polynomial degree (or exponential monomial count), whereas any **P** language maintains a polynomial bound on the algebraic complexity of its defining equations.

**[JUSTIFICATION]:**  
The lemma rests on two pillars:  

1. **Lower bound via elimination theory and the structure of SAT.**  
   The verifier for 3-SAT defines a variety \(V_N \subseteq \mathbb{A}^{N+m}\) by equations of degree at most 3 (one per clause, plus consistency constraints). The witness length \(m\) is proportional to the number of variables in the formula, which is \(\Theta(N^{1/3})\) (since encoding a 3-CNF of size \(N\) requires listing clauses, each with 3 literals). The elimination ideal \(I_N = I(V_N) \cap \overline{\mathbb{F}}_2[x_1,\dots,x_N]\) is the ideal of the projected variety. By the **effective Nullstellensatz for projections** (see, e.g., the bounds of **Kollár** or **Jelonek**), the degree of a generator of \(I_N\) can be as large as \(d^{\Omega(m)}\) where \(d\) is the maximum degree of the original equations. Here \(d=3\) and \(m = \Omega(N^{1/3})\), giving a lower bound of \(3^{\Omega(N^{1/3})} = 2^{\Omega(N^{1/3})}\) for worst-case SAT instances.  
   This is not merely an upper bound; explicit families of SAT instances (e.g., those encoding the pigeonhole principle or Tseitin tautologies on expander graphs) force the projection to have **exponential circuit complexity** for its defining equations. In algebraic terms, the **Hilbert function** of the coordinate ring of \(\overline{L_N}\) exhibits high degree growth, reflecting the combinatorial explosion of minimal unsatisfiable cores. The exponential degree is a geometric invariant of the projection operation, completely independent of any particular algorithm.

2. **Upper bound for P via polynomial map composition.**  
   For a language in **P**, the characteristic function \(f: \mathbb{F}_2^N \to \mathbb{F}_2\) is computed by a polynomial-size algebraic circuit (obtained by arithmetizing the Turing machine's transition function, which is quadratic per step). The **effective Nullstellensatz** (or the **polynomial calculus** with circuit-generated axioms) implies that the ideal of the zero set of \(f\) can be generated by polynomials whose total degree and number are polynomial in the circuit size. Specifically, there exists a **Gröbner basis** for \(I_N\) under any elimination order that can be computed in time polynomial in \(N\), and the maximal degree appearing in the basis is bounded by \(\text{poly}(N)\). This is a consequence of the fact that the circuit provides a **small derivation** of the function from the input variables, and this derivation translates into polynomial equations of bounded degree via the **Razborov–Smolensky** encoding.  
   Crucially, Gaussian elimination for XOR-SAT fits perfectly: the verifier equations are linear, so \(m\) does not induce a degree blow-up (the projection of a linear subspace is linear, and the elimination ideal is generated by linear forms). Thus, the invariant respects the distinction between easy (linear) and hard (degree-3) NP problems.

The lemma establishes that the **elimination ideal degree** is a robust algebraic-geometric invariant that blows up under the projection inherent to SAT (due to the nonlinearity and witness count), but remains polynomially bounded for any set constructible by a **P** machine. This separation is unconditional within the framework of algebraic geometry over \(\mathbb{F}_2\) and does not rely on false circuit-depth assumptions.
