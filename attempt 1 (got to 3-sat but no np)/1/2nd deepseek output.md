**[FRAMEWORK]:**  
The problem is modeled as a **3-uniform hypergraph** \( H = (V, E) \), where vertices \( V \) represent the \( N \) delegates and each hyperedge \( e \in E \) is a triple of delegates corresponding to one "Demand." A seating assignment at the Main Table corresponds to a **hitting set** (or vertex cover) \( M \subseteq V \) such that \( M \cap e \neq \emptyset \) for every \( e \in E \). The goal is to analyze the existence, minimal cardinality, and structure of such hitting sets. I will employ combinatorial hypergraph theory, focusing on connectivity, degree sequences, and structural decompositions—distinct from any algebraic or logical SAT-based approach.

**[PROPOSED STEP 1]:**  
**Lemma 1 (Connected Component Decomposition).**  
Let the hypergraph \( H \) be partitioned into connected components \( H_1, H_2, \dots, H_k \), where connectivity is defined by the existence of a path of vertices sharing hyperedges. Then a valid seating arrangement exists if and only if there exists a hitting set \( M_i \) for each component \( H_i \). Moreover, the overall minimal hitting set size satisfies \( \tau(H) = \sum_{i=1}^k \tau(H_i) \), and the problem completely decouples across components.

**[JUSTIFICATION]:**  
Since distinct connected components share neither vertices (delegates) nor hyperedges (Demands), the constraints imposed by demands within one component are entirely independent of those in another. Any choice of Main Table delegates in one component does not affect the satisfaction of demands in any other component. Thus, the existence of a global solution reduces to the existence of local solutions per component, and the minimum number of Main Table seats required is the sum of the minima for each component. This decomposition is mathematically rigorous because the hypergraph's incidence structure isolates the subproblems. It provides a foundational, non-algebraic reduction step that will later allow us to apply graph-theoretic algorithms (e.g., greedy on bounded-degree components, dynamic programming on tree-like structures) independently to each piece. This stands in stark contrast to Alpha's likely use of Boolean algebra or clause-set simplification.
