**[NOVEL FRAMEWORK]:**  
We model the **2D Cook–Levin Tableau** $T_M(\phi)$ as a **Discrete Gauge Cobordism** over a principal $G$-bundle, where the structure group $G$ is the **Monodromy Group** of the constraint sheaf over the space of partial assignments (a finite but exponentially large subgroup of the symmetric group on partial assignments). A **connection** on this bundle assigns to each 1‑cell in $T_M(\phi)$ an element of $G$ representing the transformation of the partial assignment induced by traversing that edge. The local 3‑neighborhood 2‑cells encode the **gauge transformation law**: the holonomy around the boundary of a 2‑cell must equal the identity in $G$. This is precisely the condition that the deterministic local update rule of the Turing machine is consistent with the constraint structure.  

The initial row $t=0$ carries a prescribed connection with nontrivial **Topological Charge** $Q \in H^2(T_M(\phi), \partial; \mathbb{Z})$, measured by the second Chern class of the bundle over the tableau. For a satisfiable expander instance $\phi$, Alpha has shown that the initial configuration (the empty assignment) corresponds to a connection with topological charge $Q_{in} = 2^{\Omega(n)}$. A satisfying assignment at $t=T$ corresponds to a **Flat Connection** (trivial holonomy), i.e., topological charge $Q_{out} = 0$.  

The Turing machine's execution is a **discrete Yang–Mills gradient flow**: at each time step, the machine updates the tape according to the local 2‑cells, which act as **gauge transformations** that modify the connection. The gauge group is local: a transformation at position $p$ affects only the edges incident to the 3‑neighborhood.

**[PROPOSED STEP 6]:**  
We prove the **Gauge Dissipation Bound**. In a discrete 2D gauge theory with strictly local gauge transformations (radius 1), the change in topological charge per time step is bounded by the **local energy fluctuation** across the spatial cut where the transformation occurs. Specifically, the topological charge $Q$ is an integral of a curvature 2‑form over the tableau. Because each local gauge transformation modifies the curvature only within a ball of radius $O(1)$ in the spatial direction, the total variation of $Q$ in one time step satisfies  
$$|\Delta Q| \le \sum_{p \in \text{active region}} \|\text{curvature at } p\| \le c \cdot (\text{number of active cells}).$$  
The machine head can alter at most one tape cell per step, so the number of active cells is $O(1)$. Hence $|\Delta Q| = O(1)$ per time step.  

Over $T$ time steps, the total possible change in topological charge is at most $O(T)$. Since we require $Q(T) = 0$ starting from $Q(0) = 2^{\Omega(n)}$, we must have  
$$T \ge \frac{Q(0)}{O(1)} = 2^{\Omega(n)}.$$  
Thus, any deterministic Turing machine that reduces the initial topological charge to zero (i.e., finds a satisfying assignment) requires exponential time. This establishes $\mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{NP}$ for this class of instances.

**[BARRIER EVASION CHECK]:**  
- **Relativization (Baker–Gill–Solovay):** An oracle machine $M^O$ introduces **global instantons**—non‑local hyper‑2‑cells (wormholes) that connect distant regions of the tableau in a single step. These instantons are gauge transformations that can change the topological charge by an arbitrary amount in one step, bypassing the local dissipation bound. In a relativized world, an oracle call acts as a tunneling event that instantly flattens the connection, allowing $T = \text{poly}(n)$. Since our lower bound depends on the strictly local nature of gauge transformations in the unrelativized tableau, and this locality is destroyed by oracle wormholes, the proof is **non‑relativizing**.  
- **Natural Proofs:** The hard instances are defined by the expander property of the incidence graph, a syntactic property, not a property of the Boolean function computed (satisfiable instances compute the constant‑1 function). The set is sparse and recognizing it is computationally intractable. Thus the argument evades the largeness and constructivity criteria of Razborov–Rudich.
