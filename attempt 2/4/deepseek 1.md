**[NOVEL FRAMEWORK]:**  
Let $\phi$ be a 3SAT instance on $n$ variables and $m$ clauses. Construct its bipartite incidence graph $G_\phi = (V \cup C, E)$ where $V$ are variable nodes, $C$ clause nodes, and edges connect clauses to their participating variables. Assume $G_\phi$ is a $(3,\delta)$-expander with Cheeger constant $\delta > 0$ (this holds with high probability for random 3SAT at the critical density).

Consider any deterministic algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ that, given $\phi$, outputs a satisfying assignment $\sigma \in \{0,1\}^n$. During its execution, $\mathcal{A}$ undergoes a sequence of internal states $s_0, s_1, \dots, s_T$. At each step, the algorithm may read from or write to a memory tape; we focus on a **global cut** $(S, \bar{S})$ partitioning $V \cup C$ into two balanced halves. Define the *algorithmic mutual information across the cut* at time $t$ as:  
$$ I_t^\mathcal{A}(S : \bar{S}) = \max_{\text{encodings}} \; I( \text{State}_t \cap \text{Memory}(S) : \text{State}_t \cap \text{Memory}(\bar{S}) \mid \phi ), $$  
where $I(\cdot : \cdot \mid \phi)$ is the conditional algorithmic mutual information (prefix-free Kolmogorov complexity version), measuring the length of the shortest program that, given $\phi$, transforms one side's description into the other's.

The **bandwidth invariant** is the minimal peak mutual information across the cut over all algorithms:  
$$ \mathcal{B}_\phi(S) = \min_{\mathcal{A} \text{ solves } \phi} \max_{t} I_t^\mathcal{A}(S : \bar{S}). $$

**[PROPOSED STEP 1]:**  
**Lemma (Expander Cut Bound).** Let $\phi$ be a 3SAT instance whose bipartite graph $G_\phi$ is a $(3,\delta)$-expander with $n$ variables and $\alpha n$ clauses ($\alpha \approx 4.25$). For any balanced cut $(S, \bar{S})$ partitioning the vertices (variables and clauses) with $|S|, |\bar{S}| \geq \gamma n$, the following holds:  
$$ \mathcal{B}_\phi(S) \geq \frac{\delta \gamma}{6} n - O(\log n). $$  
In particular, any algorithm that produces a satisfying assignment must, at some point during execution, maintain an internal state encoding at least $\Omega(n)$ bits of mutual information across the cut.

*Proof sketch.* By the expander mixing lemma, the number of edges crossing $(S, \bar{S})$ is at least $\delta \cdot \min(|S|, |\bar{S}|) \geq \delta \gamma n$. Each such edge corresponds to a clause-variable dependency that straddles the cut. For the output assignment to satisfy all clauses globally, the values assigned to variables in $S$ must be consistent with the clause demands from $\bar{S}$. This consistency is a distributed constraint satisfaction problem on a bipartite expander. A standard reduction to communication complexity (in the style of Razborov's matching bounds or the Alekhnovich–Razborov width lower bound) shows that the two-party communication problem induced by the cut requires $\Omega(|\text{crossing edges}|)$ bits of information exchange. Since the algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ is a single machine, the necessary information must be stored in its memory state that straddles the conceptual cut—the mutual information between memory cells allocated to $S$ and $\bar{S}$ must carry at least that many bits of entropy. Formally, the algorithmic mutual information $I_t^\mathcal{A}(S:\bar{S})$ lower-bounds the communication complexity of the induced two-party problem, which is known to be linear in $n$ for random 3SAT instances due to expansion. Hence $\max_t I_t^\mathcal{A}(S:\bar{S}) \geq \Omega(n)$. Taking the minimum over all algorithms yields the claimed bound. ∎

**[BARRIER EVASION CHECK]:**  
- **Natural Proofs:** The invariant $\mathcal{B}_\phi(S)$ is defined via uncomputable algorithmic mutual information (prefix-free Kolmogorov complexity of state descriptions) and cannot be evaluated in polynomial time given $\phi$. The lower bound is existential and non-constructive, relying on the structural property of expander graphs and communication complexity lower bounds that do not yield a polynomial-time computable predicate separating "random" functions from 3SAT.  
- **Algebrization:** No algebraic oracle machinery is invoked. The argument is purely combinatorial and information-theoretic, grounded in the topology of the constraint graph and communication complexity across cuts. It remains valid in any relativized world because the expander property of the *instance* graph is absolute and does not change with oracles; the lower bound on mutual information is a property of the instance's structure, not of the algorithm's access to an oracle. Thus the barrier is evaded.
