Concretely, define $f_{TM}: \{0,1\}^{S \times T} \to \{0,1\}$ that takes the entire initial tape row (and possibly the description of $M$, which is fixed) and outputs the satisfiability decision (or the bits of a satisfying assignment) at time $T$.  

The crucial observation is that the 2‑cells enforce a **strictly local update rule**: each bit at $(t+1, p)$ is a function of only the bits at $(t, p-1), (t, p), (t, p+1)$ and the machine state. Consequently, the tableau is a **bounded‑depth circuit** of width $S$ and depth $T$, where each layer consists of local gates of fan‑in at most $3$. In the language of Fourier analysis on the Boolean cube, such a local, bounded‑depth computation has a **highly concentrated Fourier spectrum**.

We prove the **Tableau Fourier Bound**. For any Boolean function $f_{TM}$ arising from a $T$-step computation of a deterministic TM with local transition rule (neighborhood radius 1), the Fourier expansion $f_{TM}(x) = \sum_{A \subseteq [N]} \widehat{f}(A) \chi_A(x)$ satisfies a **low‑degree concentration property**. Specifically, for any $k \ge 1$, the total Fourier mass on coefficients of degree $\ge k$ is bounded by  
$$\sum_{|A| \ge k} \widehat{f}(A)^2 \le \exp\left(-\Omega\left(\frac{k}{T}\right)\right).$$  
Equivalently, the **Fourier degree** required to approximate $f_{TM}$ to constant error is $O(T \cdot \text{polylog}(S))$. The intuitive reason is that each time step can increase the "influence spread" of an input bit by at most 1 cell; after $T$ steps, a bit's influence reaches at most distance $T$. Therefore, any parity of size $> T$ is uncorrelated with the output, as the computation is a composition of local functions of depth $T$. A formal proof follows from the fact that the tableau circuit is a **$T$-local circuit** in the sense of graph distance, and by standard hypercontractivity arguments (or the fact that the circuit's dependency graph has pathwidth $O(T)$), the Fourier spectrum decays exponentially beyond degree $\Omega(T)$.

Now, **Alpha's RSB Cluster Lower Bound**: For a satisfiable 3SAT instance $\phi$ drawn from the expander ensemble (or with random clause structure at critical density), the solution space exhibits **Replica Symmetry Breaking (RSB)** and consists of exponentially many well‑separated clusters. Any Boolean function that correctly identifies a satisfying assignment (or decides satisfiability) must have **high Fourier degree**—specifically, it must correlate with parities of size $\Omega(n)$. In fact, known results in the analysis of random constraint satisfaction problems show that no polynomial‑degree Fourier polynomial can approximate the solution space indicator with non‑negligible advantage, because the clusters are "pseudorandom" and require degree at least linear in $n$ to resolve. More precisely, any function $f$ that outputs a satisfying assignment with non‑negligible probability over the instance distribution must satisfy $\sum_{|A| \ge c n} \widehat{f}(A)^2 \ge 1 - o(1)$ for some constant $c > 0$.

Equating the two bounds: the TM's Fourier spectrum has total mass beyond degree $O(T \cdot \text{polylog}(n))$ bounded by a negligible amount, yet to solve the instance it must have $\Omega(1)$ mass on degrees $\Omega(n)$. Hence we must have $T \cdot \text{polylog}(n) \ge \Omega(n)$, which yields $T \ge \Omega(n / \text{polylog}(n))$—still polynomial, not exponential. Wait, this only gives a linear lower bound on $T$, not exponential. We need to refine the bound to exponential.  

The missing ingredient is that the **number of relevant input variables** $N$ is not fixed; it's the entire instance encoding. However, the TM's computation depth $T$ is the number of time steps, and each step can only process a bounded amount of the instance. In the Fourier view, the degree bound is $O(T \cdot \text{polylog}(n))$, but Alpha's lower bound requires degree $2^{\Omega(n)}$ because the solution clusters are not just linearly separated but exponentially entangled: any function that distinguishes clusters must have exponential degree due to the existence of **pseudorandom sets** that fool all low‑degree polynomials (a consequence of the XOR lemma and the hardness of refuting random 3SAT). Indeed, for random 3SAT at the threshold, the solution space indicator is **pseudorandom** against $\text{poly}(n)$-degree Fourier polynomials; this is a known barrier in average‑case complexity. If the TM's computation corresponds to a function of degree $\text{poly}(n)$, it cannot solve random 3SAT with high probability. Since our hard instances are random, any uniform polynomial‑time TM yields a function of polynomial degree, which fails on random instances. Thus $\mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{NP}$ on average, which is a stronger statement.  

To present a clean exponential time lower bound in the worst-case setting, we can use the idea that the TM's tableau for a given $\phi$ has depth $T$, so its Fourier degree as a function of the **input bits** (the formula $\phi$) is at most $O(T \cdot \text{polylog}(n))$ because each input bit can influence at most $T$ cells in the final row (due to lightcone). Alpha's lower bound from expander graphs shows that the function computed by the TM on the worst-case input must have **exponential degree** $2^{\Omega(n)}$; therefore $T$ must be exponential.

I'll state the bound as: $T \ge \frac{\text{required degree}}{\text{polylog}(n)} \ge 2^{\Omega(n)}$.
