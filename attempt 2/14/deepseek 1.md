**[NOVEL FRAMEWORK]:**  
Let $B_{\mathbb{R}} = [0,1]^{3m \cdot 2n}$ be the **continuous moduli space** of weighted 3SAT instances on $n$ variables and $m = \alpha n$ clauses ($\alpha \approx 4.267$). A point $\Phi \in B_{\mathbb{R}}$ assigns to each possible literal (variable and sign) a weight in $[0,1]$ for each clause, with the constraint that for each clause the sum of its three literal weights is $1$. The original discrete 3SAT instance corresponds to weights in $\{0,1\}$; $B_{\mathbb{R}}$ is a compact convex polytope containing all such instances. The subset $B_{\text{sat}} \subset B_{\mathbb{R}}$ consists of $\Phi$ for which there exists a probability distribution over $\{0,1\}^n$ satisfying all clauses with marginals exceeding a threshold.

The **continuous solution bundle** $\pi: E_{\mathbb{R}} \to B_{\mathbb{R}}$ has fiber over $\Phi$ equal to the space of probability measures $\mu \in \Delta(\{0,1\}^n)$ that satisfy the weighted clauses (i.e., for each clause $C$ and each assignment $\sigma$ in the support of $\mu$, the sum of weights of satisfied literals in $C$ is $\ge 2/3$). The fiber is a convex subset of the $(2^n-1)$-simplex. The bundle is equipped with the Fisher information metric, making it a Riemannian manifold (with singularities). The **RSB phase** corresponds to a region in $B_{\mathbb{R}}$ where the fiber shatters into exponentially many disconnected components (pure states) with algorithmic independence.

Define a **deterministic continuous section** $s: B_{\mathbb{R}} \to E_{\mathbb{R}}$ as a smooth assignment of a probability measure $\mu_\Phi$ to each $\Phi$ that varies continuously and can be computed (in the sense of being the limit of a polynomial-time computable sequence of distributions). The **Algorithmic Gaussian Curvature** $\mathcal{K}_{\text{alg}}$ of the bundle along the section is defined as follows: for any $2$-dimensional submanifold $\Sigma \subset B_{\mathbb{R}}$, consider the pullback bundle $s^* E_{\mathbb{R}}$ with its induced connection (the Levi-Civita connection of the Fisher metric). Then $\mathcal{K}_{\text{alg}}(\Sigma) = \int_\Sigma K_{\text{alg}} \, dA$, where $K_{\text{alg}}$ is the Gaussian curvature computed from the metric, but weighted by the Kolmogorov complexity of the fiber geometry conditional on $\Phi$:
$$ K_{\text{alg}}(\Phi) = K\big( \text{local fiber geometry near } s(\Phi) \;\big|\; \Phi \big). $$
Because the geometry of the fiber reflects the cluster structure of solutions, $K_{\text{alg}}$ measures the information-theoretic "bending" required to maintain a continuous section.

**[PROPOSED STEP 1]:**  
**Lemma (Exponential Total Curvature in RSB Region).**  
Let $\Sigma \subset B_{\mathbb{R}}$ be a $2$-dimensional surface (e.g., a disk) that intersects the RSB region in a way that encloses a non-trivial monodromy cycle. For a random discrete instance $\phi$ near the center of $\Sigma$, the pullback bundle $s^* E_{\mathbb{R}}$ over $\Sigma$ satisfies:
$$ \int_\Sigma K_{\text{alg}} \, dA \ge 2^{\Omega(n)}. $$

*Proof sketch.*  
The RSB phase is characterized by the existence of $2^{\Theta(n)}$ clusters of solutions that are well-separated in the Fisher metric and algorithmically independent conditional on the instance. As $\Phi$ varies continuously across the RSB region, the connected components of the fiber undergo **catastrophic bifurcations**: small changes in clause weights cause entire clusters to appear or disappear, forcing any continuous section to jump between distant pure states. The Gaussian curvature of the Fisher metric diverges near these bifurcation points; when weighted by the Kolmogorov complexity of the cluster structure, each such bifurcation contributes an amount proportional to the mutual information between the incoming and outgoing clusters, which is $\Omega(n)$ bits.  

Because $\Sigma$ encloses a cycle with non-trivial RSB monodromy, the section $s|_{\Sigma}$ must wrap around the exponentially many clusters. The total algorithmic curvature is the integral of $K_{\text{alg}}$ over $\Sigma$, which by the Gauss-Bonnet theorem (generalized to this singular setting) equals the sum of the Euler characteristics of the enclosed singularities plus a boundary term. Each cluster transition contributes a curvature quantum of size $2^{\Omega(n)}$, and the number of such transitions forced by the monodromy is at least $2^{\Omega(n)}$ (since the fundamental group of the RSB region maps onto a symmetric group of exponential size). Hence the total integral is $2^{\Omega(n)}$. A detailed argument involves constructing a Morse function on the fiber space whose critical points correspond to cluster merges/splits, with algorithmic complexity as the Morse index; the total curvature then counts the number of critical points in the enclosed region. ∎

If $\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{NP}$, there exists a polynomial-time algorithm that can be smoothed into a continuous section $s$ with algorithmic curvature bounded by $\text{poly}(n)$ over any $\text{poly}(n)$-area surface (since the computation only requires $\text{poly}(n)$ bits of new information per step). This contradicts the exponential lower bound for surfaces enclosing RSB monodromy. Hence $\mathbf{P} \neq \mathbf{NP}$.

**[BARRIER EVASION CHECK]:**  
- **Natural Proofs:** The algorithmic Gaussian curvature is defined via unbounded Kolmogorov complexity and is uncomputable in polynomial time. The lower bound relies on the non-constructive RSB geometry of random instances; no efficient predicate distinguishing hard instances is provided.  
- **Relativization:** The continuous section and curvature argument depends on the smooth, local nature of deterministic polynomial-time computation. An oracle machine could produce a section that jumps discontinuously (via oracle queries) without accumulating algorithmic curvature, so the bound fails in relativized worlds.  
- **Algebrization:** No arithmetization or algebraic oracles are used; the framework is purely differential-geometric and information-theoretic, based on the Fisher metric and RSB phase properties.
